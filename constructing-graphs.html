What is a frequency table?

A frequency table is a way to organise data. It has two columns: one for the categories (types of food, colours, names…) and one for the count — how many times each category appears.

The category column lists the groups. The count column shows how often each one appears.
Big idea 2

What is a column graph?

A column graph (or bar graph) shows data as vertical columns. The taller the column, the bigger the value.
Columns have spaces between them because the categories are separate groups.

The horizontal line is the x-axis. The vertical line going up is the y-axis.

Every column graph needs…

A graph is only finished when someone else can read it without asking you a single question.

🏷️ Title

Tells us what the graph is about.

e.g. “Our class’s favourite pets”.

↔️ Horizontal axis label

Names the categories.

e.g. Pet, Food, Month, Material.

↕️ Vertical axis label

Names what is being measured.

e.g. Number of students.

📏 Scale

Even numbers up the vertical axis.

e.g. 0, 1, 2, 3, 4, 5…

▮▮ Equal column widths

All columns the same width, with equal gaps.

🔎 Readable

Can a stranger read it with no help? Then it’s done.

Big idea 3

Choosing a scale

Sometimes the numbers are too big to plot 1 square = 1 unit.
Then we use a many-to-one scale — each square stands for more than one unit. Look at your largest value to decide.

Steps to create a column graph

  1. Read the frequency table and find the largest value — this sets your scale.
  2. Draw the axes — horizontal (across) and vertical (up).
  3. Label your horizontal axis with the category names.
  4. Label your vertical axis and mark the scale evenly.
  5. Draw a column for each category up to its value. Keep columns equal width with equal gaps.
  6. Write a title at the top of your graph.
  7. Check your graph — can someone else read it without asking you questions?

Interpret your graph

  1. Which food was the most popular? How do you know?
  2. Which foods were equally popular?
  3. How many more students prefer Pizza over Curry?
✓ Answers
  • Bars: Burger 8 · Curry 5 · Pasta 5 · Pizza 10.
  • Most popular: Pizza — it has the tallest column (10 students).
  • Equally popular: Curry and Pasta — both 5 students (equal height columns).
  • Pizza vs Curry: 10 − 5 = 5 more students prefer Pizza.
Teacher“How do you know?” is the key question — push students to justify from the height of the column, not memory.
🟠 Amber You decide the title, labels & scale Two scenarios

Scenario 1 — Rainfall Over Winter

Sally recorded the rain over winter: June 6 cm, July 7 cm, August 8 cm. Type your own title and axis names into the fields, pick the scale, then build the graph.

Scenario 2 — Walkathon Fundraiser

Friends walked for 2 weeks: Josie 10 km, Heather 22 km, Jose 15 km, Maxwell 9 km, Mya 18 km. Build your own frequency table (in your book), decide the scale, then build the graph.

Interpret your graphs

  1. In which month did it rain the most? What does the graph show?
  2. Who walked the furthest? How much further did they walk than the person who walked the least?
✓ Answers & guidance
  • Rainfall table: category = Month, count = Rainfall (cm). Largest value 8 → 1 square = 1 unit works well. Title e.g. “Rainfall Over Winter”.
  • Rainiest month: August — it has the tallest column (8 cm).
  • Walkathon: largest value 22 → a scale of 1 square = 5 units keeps the graph a sensible size. Axis labels: Person and Distance (km).
  • Furthest: Heather (22 km). Least: Maxwell (9 km). Difference = 22 − 9 = 13 km further.
TeacherUse the Scale dropdown as the teaching point: with 1 square = 1 unit the walkathon graph gets very tall (22 squares!). Ask “which scale is easier to read?” → 1 square = 5.
🔴 Red Worded problems · design everything Extract → scale → graph

1 · Water Absorption Experiment

A class tested how many drops of water materials absorbed before dripping: paper towel 18, tissue 10, sponge 24, cotton fabric 14, foil 0. Suggested: title “Water Absorbed by Different Materials”, horizontal axis “Material”, vertical axis “Drops absorbed”.

2 · Plant Growth Experiment

Bean plant heights after two weeks: sunlight 15 cm, shade 8 cm, no water 2 cm, extra water 12 cm, near the window 14 cm. Include a title and both axis labels.

3 · Magnet Investigation

Paperclips each magnet picked up: A 12, B 20, C 8, D 15, E 25. Show the strength of each magnet.

✓ Answers & guidance
  • Water: bars 18, 10, 24, 14, 0. Largest 24 → 1 square = 5. Note foil = 0, so its column has no height — a great discussion point.
  • Plants: bars 15, 8, 2, 12, 14. Largest 15 → 1 square = 5 (or 1 square = 2). Tallest = sunlight; shortest = no water.
  • Magnets: bars 12, 20, 8, 15, 25. Largest 25 → 1 square = 5. Strongest = Magnet E (25); weakest = Magnet C (8).
  • All three: every graph needs a title, a labelled horizontal axis, a labelled vertical axis, an even scale, and equal-width columns with equal gaps.
TeacherLook for students choosing the scale before drawing, and handling the foil = 0 case. Extend: ask them to write one question a reader could answer from their graph.
