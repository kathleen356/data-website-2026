1 The data family tree

Every piece of data has a family. To find out where it belongs, ask two questions: 1) Is it a word or a number? Then 2) what kind?

DATA

🏷️ Categorical

Words, labels or groups. You can't do maths with them.

NominalNo natural order. Eye colour, favourite sport.
OrdinalCategories you can rank. Movie ratings, sizes.

🔢 Numerical

Numbers you can count or measure. You can do maths with them.

DiscreteWhole-number jumps. Students in a class, goals scored.
ContinuousAny value on a scale. Weight, height, time.

2 Categorical data: nominal vs ordinal

Nominal data has no natural order — you can't say one label is "more" than another. Ordinal data can be ranked from lowest to highest, even though it's still words.

Drag each card into the bin where it belongs.

A survey asks students to pick their favourite house group (Blackwood, Clarke, Hensley, Mungo, and Taylor). Is this nominal or ordinal?
💡 There's no order to house colours — none is "higher" than another — so it's nominal.

3 Numerical data: discrete vs continuous

Discrete data is counted — it jumps in whole numbers with nothing sensible in between (you can't have 2.5 pets). Continuous data is measured — it can take any value on a scale, including the bits in between.

Drag each card into the bin where it belongs.

A stopwatch records how long it takes a runner to finish a lap. Is this discrete or continuous?
💡 Time can be any value — 21.4 seconds, 21.43 seconds — so it's continuous.

Part 2 — Answer these questions in one to two full sentences Amber

Write your answers in your maths book, then use the self-check to mark your own work.

1. A school records how long (in minutes and seconds) it takes each student to complete a 200m run. What type of data is this, and why?
Self-check ✓
A strong answer says it is numerical → continuous, because time is measured and can take any value on a scale — including in-between values like 1 minute 32.6 seconds.
2. Mrs Baines surveys Year 5 about their house group (Taylor, Hensley, Clarke, Blackwood and Mungo). Classify this data and explain your reasoning.
Self-check ✓
A strong answer says it is categorical → nominal, because house groups are names/labels and there is no natural order — no house is "higher" than another.
3. A class votes for their preferred fruit: apple, banana, mango, or strawberry. Classify this data and explain whether it is nominal or ordinal.
Self-check ✓
A strong answer says it is categorical → nominal. The fruits are labels that can't be ranked from lowest to highest, so the data is nominal, not ordinal.

Part 3 — Explain your thinking clearly Red

Write your answers in your maths book, then use the self-check to mark your own work.

1. A weather station collects the following data each day: the date, temperature (°C), rainfall (mm), wind speed (km/h), and a weather description (sunny, cloudy, rainy, stormy). Classify each piece of data as discrete, continuous, nominal, or ordinal, and justify each classification.
Self-check ✓
A strong answer classifies all five: date → ordinal (dates have a natural order); temperature → continuous, rainfall → continuous and wind speed → continuous (all measured on a scale with in-between values possible); weather description → nominal (labels with no natural order).
2. A researcher argues that star ratings (1 star to 5 stars) on a restaurant review app are discrete data because they are whole numbers. Another researcher says they are ordinal data. Who is correct, and why?
Self-check ✓
A strong answer sides with ordinal: the stars are really ranked categories (worst → best), and the numbers are just labels for the ranks — the gap between 1★ and 2★ isn't necessarily the same "amount" as between 4★ and 5★. It can also acknowledge the first researcher noticed something true (ratings jump in whole steps), but counting whole numbers alone doesn't make data numerical.
3. A student claims: "Shoe sizes must be continuous data because feet are measured." Evaluate this claim. Is it fully correct, partially correct, or incorrect? Justify your answer.
Self-check ✓
A strong answer says partially correct: foot length is continuous (measured, any value possible), but shoe sizes only come in set steps (6, 6.5, 7…) with nothing available in between — so shoe size itself behaves like discrete data even though it comes from a measurement.
